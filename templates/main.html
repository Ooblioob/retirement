Step 2: Learn tips to make your claiming decision

Tell us about your lifestyle for personalized information.

Are you married?

The age you claim will affect your benefits, as well as your spouse’s.

If you or your spouse wait until full retirement age to claim, you will have claiming options such as “file and suspend” and “restricted application”. Additionally, if you are the higher earner in the household, it’s particularly important to wait until full retirement age because your spouse will receive a higher survivor benefit.

The longer you delay claiming, the higher your monthly Social Security benefit will be.

Because you’re unmarried, your claiming decision is simpler. For every year that you wait until age 70, you will get a permanent increase in your benefits, protecting you from running out of money in retirement.

Do you plan on working in your 60s?

Stay on track for a secure retirement.

Working longer will help you maximize your Social Security benefits. Because your benefit is based on your highest 35 years of earnings, working longer can add higher earnings to your record by replacing years with low earnings. This might be especially helpful if there were years you were a student, were unemployed, or were taking time off to care for someone. When you work and wait to claim, you can nearly double your benefits by age 70!

Consider working fewer hours or changing careers.

Waiting to claim increases your monthly benefits every year until age 70. Working full-time is not the only option in your 60s. Many people plan a career change or work part-time. If you stop or reduce your work hours, it’s still possible to increase your Social Security benefits by waiting to claim. You could use a portion of your 401(k) or other retirement savings to supplement your earnings. You’ll need to decide whether doing this is financially possible.

Will your expenses decrease after you retire?

Some people find retirement is more expensive than expected.

You’re likely to spend 20 years in retirement and income needs are difficult to predict. During that time, you’ll need savings to pay for expenses like home repairs, medical bills, or even buying a car. Out-of-pocket health care costs will likely increase as you get older. You can make adjustments, like downsizing or paying off your mortgage before you retire. You can also wait to claim Social Security up to age 70, which increases your benefits, creating a buffer against future increases in expenses.

Will you have sources of income in addition to Social Security benefits?

Continue to save for a secure retirement.

Social Security was never intended to be your only source of retirement income. That means your other sources, like savings, a 401(k) or an IRA will need to fill the gap. Waiting to claim your benefits (up to age 70) will ensure that this gap is as small as possible. If you claim before your full retirement age, your monthly benefit is permanently reduced by as much as 30 percent. Claiming early means that you will need to save more on your own for a secure retirement.

Take advantage of catch-up contributions.

Social Security was never intended to be your only source of retirement income. That means your other sources will need to fill this gap. Two steps you can take to ensure that this gap is as small as possible: 1) wait to claim your Social Security benefits (up to age 70) and 2) after age 50, take advantage of 401(k) annual catch-up contributions.

Did you know retirement savings plans including 401(k)s come with penalties if you take your money out before age 59½? Consider accessing your 401(k) assets later in retirement.

You have time and options to save.

You could save through your employer’s retirement plan, IRA or myRA. Make sure to take an employer match if one is offered. We understand you may not be able to save as much as you’d like, so your decision about when to claim Social Security benefits is even more important. Waiting to claim as long as possible (up to age 70) can nearly double your monthly benefit.

Do you expect to live a long time?

Delaying claiming of your Social Security benefits is the best way to protect yourself in old age.

The average life expectancy is 84 for men and 87 for women. Because Social Security provides lifetime guaranteed income, it is especially valuable to you in your 80s and beyond. Waiting to claim your Social Security benefits is the best way to cover expenses if you live a long time. Your benefit increases the longer you wait to claim, up to age 70, and are adjusted every year to keep up with the cost of living.

There’s a good chance that you may live into your 80s.
